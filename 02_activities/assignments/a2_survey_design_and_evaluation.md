The 2018 GSS GVP collected data from persons aged 15 years and over living in private households in Canada, excluding residents of the Yukon, Northwest Territories and Nunavut and full time residents of institutions.
1. Sample type: It is a cross-sectional survey collecting data at a single point in time. The sample is based on a stratified design employing probability sampling. The stratification is done at the province/census metropolitan area (CMA) level. 

2. Sample size: The sample included 16,149 respondents aged 15 and above. According to the documents, a field sample of approximatively 50,000 units was used. Among them, about 40,000 invitation letters to the electronic questionnaire were sent to selected households across Canada. A completion of 24,000 questionnaires was expected. 

3. Target population: All individuals aged 15 and above living in private households in 10 provinces of Canada, excluding full-time (residing for more than six months) residents of institutions.

4. Sampling frame: The frame for GSS was created using several linked sources, such as the Census, administrative data and billing files.The sampling frame uses address and telephone registers to generate a list of households eligible for selection. The methodological design integrates multiple sources (e.g., telephone number listings and address registers) to cover households across Canada.

5. Survey mode(s): The survey was administered using online electronic questionnaire, or through CATI (computer assisted telephone interviewing). It was the first time that GSS used an online option for the respondents. The respondents could choose between French and English and the average time to complete the survey was estimated at 44 minutes.

6. Timeline: Data collection for this reference period: 2018-09-04 to 2018-12-28

7. Response rate: The overall response rate is 41.9%

8. Weights: Survey weights were used to adjust for non-response and to ensure estimates are representative of the target population.
Additional adjustments used administrative data (e.g., income, household composition) to reduce non-response bias. Weights ensure survey estimates represent the official target population

9. Data processing: Data was collected via telephone interviews or self-completed electronic questionnaires. Responses were edited and coded for consistency. Bootstrap weights were generated to estimate sampling variability, and survey weights applied to adjust for non-response and coverage error.

10. Cleaning, imputation, etc: Except for a few cases, all imputations were made using donor records selected through a score function. Each non-responding record was matched to the ‚Äúnearest‚Äù donor record based on shared characteristics. If donor-based imputation was not possible, mean imputation among donor records was used.
Imputation was carried out in 9 steps. The first step consisted of imputing personal income and family income. The next three steps involved imputing the formal volunteering variables in the master file. Steps five and six were imputing the informal volunteering variables in the master file. Finally, the last three steps involved imputing variables in the donation file and the solicitation methods in the master file.
Personal income questions were not asked in the survey but the information was obtained by linkage to the tax data for those who had not objected. Personal data of 81.9 per cent was obtained from T1FF while missing information for others was imputed. Overall a family income value was obtained for 81.7 per cent of households. Missing information for other respondents was imputed.

11. Sources of error: The methodology of this survey has been designed to limit the number of errors and to reduce their potential effects. However, the results of the survey remain subject to both sampling and non-sampling error
    Sampling error: Estimates vary across samples; bootstrap method used to quantify variability. Estimates with high sampling variability are indicated in this publication and all of the highlighted differences between subgroups of the population are significant at the 95% level.

    Non-sampling error: Common sources of these errors are imperfect coverage and non-response.
    Coverage error: Some households without telephones were excluded. Survey estimates were adjusted (weighted) to represent all persons in the target population, including those not covered by the survey frame.
    Non-response error at household and individual level was mitigated through weighting. 
    Other non-sampling errors: Efforts were made to minimize bias by used a well-tested questionnaire, a proven methodology, specialized interviews and strict quality control. 

12. Limitations, known biases, etc:
    Imperfect coverage: households without telephones excluded, residual undercoverage or overcoverage may exist
    Non-response bias: weights reduce but may not fully eliminate bias
    Low response rate: 41.9% may leave potential for bias
    Methodological changes (e.g., new Internet survey option in 2018) limit comparability with earlier cycles
